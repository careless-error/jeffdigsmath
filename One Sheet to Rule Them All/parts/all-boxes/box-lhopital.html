L’Hôpital’s Rule
If \( \displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \) gives an indeterminate form like \( \displaystyle \frac{0}{0} \) or \( \displaystyle \frac{\infty}{\infty} \),
then \( \displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \), if that new limit exists.
Indeterminate forms: \( \displaystyle \frac{0}{0},\ \frac{\infty}{\infty},\ 0\cdot\infty,\ \infty-\infty,\ 0^0,\ 1^\infty,\ \infty^0 \).
Works for one-sided limits \( \displaystyle \lim_{x\to a^-},\ \lim_{x\to a^+} \) and limits at infinity \( \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\pm\infty} \).
Use L’Hôpital’s Rule only for quotients. Other forms must be rewritten using algebra or logarithms before applying the rule.
