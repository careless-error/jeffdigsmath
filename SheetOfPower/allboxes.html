Limit Definition
\( \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\)
\(\iff \)
\( \forall \varepsilon > 0\ \exists \delta > 0 :\)
\( 0 < |x - a| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon \)

For every \( \varepsilon > 0 \), there exists \( \delta > 0 \) such that if \( 0 < |x - a| < \delta \), then \( |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon \).

No matter how tight a tolerance \( \varepsilon \) around \( L \), you can find a window \( \delta \) around \( x = a \) so that if \( x \) lies in that window (but isn't exactly \( a \)), then \( f(x) \) stays within \( \varepsilon \) of \( L \).
The Derivative: definition and differentials
\( f'(x) = \displaystyle \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x} \)

\( \Delta y = f(x + \Delta x) - f(x), \quad dy = f'(x)dx \)

The differential \( dy \) uses the derivative to estimate how much \( y \) changes for a small change \( dx \).
As \( \Delta x \to 0 \), \( \Delta y \approx dy \).

Tangent approximation:
\[ f(x + \Delta x) \approx f(x) + f'(x)\Delta x \]
Special Limits
\(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1 \quad \text{and} \quad \displaystyle \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{x} = 0 \)
Rolle’s Theorem
If \( f \) is continuous on \( [a, b] \) and differentiable on \( (a, b) \), and \( f(a) = f(b) \),
then there exists a point \( c \in (a, b) \) such that:
\( f'(c) = 0 \)
Mean Value Theorem (MVT)
If \( f \) is continuous on \( [a, b] \) and differentiable on \( (a, b) \),
then there exists a point \( c \in (a, b) \) such that:
\( f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a} \)
Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT)
If \( f \) is continuous on \( [a, b] \) and \( M \) is between \( f(a) \) and \( f(b) \),
then there exists a point \( c \in [a, b] \) such that:
\( f(c) = M \)

\[ \int f(g(x))g'(x)\,dx = \int f(u)\,du \]
For Definite Integrals: Also change the limits:
\[ \int_a^b f(g(x))g'(x)\,dx = \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u)\,du \]
Integration of Even and Odd Functions
If \( f(x) \) is even (\( f(-x) = f(x) \)), then:
\[ \int_{-a}^a f(x)\,dx = 2\int_0^a f(x)\,dx \]
If \( f(x) \) is odd (\( f(-x) = -f(x) \)), then:
\[ \int_{-a}^a f(x)\,dx = 0 \]

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
If \( f \) is continuous on \([a, b] \) and \( F' = f \), then:

\[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a) \]
The definite integral gives the total change in \( F \) over \([a, b]\).

Net Change Theorem: If \( f \) is a rate, the integral gives total accumulation or change.

Applications: displacement, total cost, accumulated quantity
Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
If \( f \) is continuous on an interval and \[ F(x) = \int_a^{g(x)} f(t)\,dt \] then \[ F'(x) = f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \] The derivative of an integral with a variable upper limit is the integrand evaluated at that limit times the derivative of the limit.
Mean Value Theorem for Integrals
If \( f \) is continuous on \([a, b] \), then there exists a number \( c \in [a, b] \) such that:

\[ f(c)(b - a) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx \]
Note: The average value of \( f \) on \([a, b] \) is given by \[ f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{b - a} \int_a^b f(x)\,dx \] so \( f(c) = f_{\text{avg}} \).
L’Hôpital’s Rule
If \( \displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \) gives an indeterminate form like \( \displaystyle \frac{0}{0} \) or \( \displaystyle \frac{\infty}{\infty} \),
then \( \displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \), if that new limit exists.
Indeterminate forms: \( \displaystyle \frac{0}{0},\ \frac{\infty}{\infty},\ 0\cdot\infty,\ \infty-\infty,\ 0^0,\ 1^\infty,\ \infty^0 \).
Works for one-sided limits \( \displaystyle \lim_{x\to a^-},\ \lim_{x\to a^+} \) and limits at infinity \( \displaystyle \lim_{x\to\pm\infty} \).
Use L’Hôpital’s Rule only for quotients. Other forms must be rewritten using algebra or logarithms before applying the rule.
